I just watched a startup burn through $2M because they chose microservices on day one.

Three engineers. Zero users. Twenty-seven different services.

Here's what I learned from watching teams make (and recover from) this architectural decision over the past decade.

Small teams (under 10 people) almost always do better with monoliths. You don't have the bandwidth to manage distributed systems complexity. I've seen 3-person teams try to run 15 microservices and it becomes a full-time job just keeping the lights on.

Microservices need automated deployments, comprehensive monitoring, distributed tracing, and on-call processes. If you don't have these already, microservices will hurt more than they help. Build these capabilities first, then consider splitting services.

Don't split services because they "feel" separate. Split them when you have evidence they should be separate. Different scaling needs, different team ownership, different release cycles. Clear business reasons, not just clean code reasons.

Keep stable, core functionality in a well-structured monolith. Extract services only for new features with unclear requirements or components with genuinely different needs. Most successful companies I've worked with use this pattern.

Start with a monolith, but design it well. Use clear module boundaries and think about how you might split it later. Extract services gradually when you have evidence they should be separate, not just because it feels architecturally pure.

The most successful teams focus on building good software first, then worry about how to structure it.
